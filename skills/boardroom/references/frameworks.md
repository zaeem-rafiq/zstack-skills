# Decision Frameworks

Apply these modifiers to each advisor's instructions when a framework is selected.

---

## standard

No special framework. Advisors deliberate freely.

---

## pre-mortem

**Advisor Modifier:**

FRAMEWORK INSTRUCTION: Assume this decision has already been implemented and failed spectacularly within 18 months. Your job is to conduct a retrospective failure analysis. Explain the most likely failure mode in concrete terms — what went wrong, what cascading effects followed, and what early warning signs were visible in hindsight but ignored at the time. Identify the single biggest blind spot that led to the failure, grounding your analysis in the specific business context, stage, runway, and competitive landscape. You should still provide your vote and projections, but frame them through the lens of "what must go right to avoid this failure" rather than optimistic planning.

**Synthesis Modifier:**

When synthesizing this pre-mortem debate, consolidate the failure scenarios into a ranked risk matrix. Identify which failure modes multiple advisors independently predicted, as these represent the highest-probability risks. Extract the most critical mitigation strategies from the advisor positions and present them as a concrete action plan the founder can execute immediately to de-risk the decision.

## swot

**Advisor Modifier:**

FRAMEWORK INSTRUCTION: Organize your entire position paper around the SWOT framework. Explicitly label four sections — Strengths, Weaknesses, Opportunities, and Threats — and provide specific, evidence-based examples from the business context for each quadrant. Do not leave any quadrant with fewer than two concrete points. Your projections should map directly to the relevant SWOT categories: estimated cost and risk assessment should tie to Weaknesses and Threats, while revenue impact should connect to Strengths and Opportunities. Your vote and conviction score should reflect the overall balance across all four quadrants.

**Synthesis Modifier:**

When synthesizing this debate, create a consolidated SWOT matrix that merges all advisor inputs. For each quadrant, identify the points that multiple advisors raised independently (high-confidence signals) versus unique observations from individual advisors. Highlight where advisors placed the same factor in different quadrants, as these disagreements reveal the most nuanced strategic tensions. The recommendation should address the top items from each quadrant.

## 10-10-10

**Advisor Modifier:**

FRAMEWORK INSTRUCTION: Evaluate this decision through three distinct time lenses. First, the immediate consequences (10 minutes/days): what happens right after this decision is executed, including team morale, cash flow impact, and operational disruption. Second, the medium-term impact (10 months): how does this reshape the business trajectory, competitive position, and key metrics over the next year. Third, the long-term trajectory (10 years): what does this decision mean for the company vision, market position, and founder legacy at scale. Each of your four projections should include analysis at all three horizons. Weight your vote and conviction score more heavily toward the 10-year consequences — short-term pain is acceptable if the long-term trajectory is strong.

## red-blue

**Advisor Modifier:**

FRAMEWORK INSTRUCTION: [TEAM_ASSIGNMENT] Regardless of your personal opinion, you must construct the strongest possible case for your assigned side. If you are Blue Team, find every reason this decision should proceed and argue forcefully for it. If you are Red Team, find every flaw, risk, and reason this decision should be rejected. Do not hedge or present a balanced view — your role is adversarial advocacy for your assigned position. Use specific data from the business context to support your case, and directly anticipate and counter the arguments the opposing team will make. Your vote must align with your team assignment (Blue = yes, Red = no), but your conviction score should reflect how strong you believe your assigned case genuinely is.

**Team Assignment Rule:** Assign the first half of advisors to Blue Team (argue FOR) and the second half to Red Team (argue AGAINST). Replace [TEAM_ASSIGNMENT] with either "You are assigned to BLUE TEAM. You must argue FOR this decision." or "You are assigned to RED TEAM. You must argue AGAINST this decision."

**Synthesis Modifier:**

When synthesizing this Red Team / Blue Team debate, evaluate both sides with strict objectivity. Determine which team constructed the stronger argument based on evidence quality, logical consistency, and grounding in the business context — not based on vote count, which is predetermined by team assignment. Identify arguments from either side that went effectively uncontested, as these represent the strongest signals. The recommendation should be based on argument merit, explicitly stating which team "won" the debate and why.

## regret-minimization

**Advisor Modifier:**

FRAMEWORK INSTRUCTION: Apply the regret minimization framework to this decision. Project yourself to age 80, looking back on your career — which choice minimizes the probability of regret? First, classify this decision as either reversible (two-way door) or irreversible (one-way door). For reversible decisions, bias strongly toward action because the cost of inaction is often invisible but real. For irreversible decisions, demand higher certainty and more evidence before committing. In your position, explicitly weigh the regret of inaction (missing the opportunity, watching competitors move, stagnating) against the regret of wrong action (wasted resources, damaged reputation, lost runway). Your conviction score should reflect how clearly one path dominates the other on the regret axis.
